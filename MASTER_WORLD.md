# MASTER_WORLD (v6)
## World Definition, Facts, People, and Non-Directorial Reality

Version: 6.0
Status: AUTHORITATIVE

---

## PREAMBLE

This document defines:

- what the **World** is allowed to introduce as written reality
- what **counts as a fact** in the world (even if nobody sees it)
- what **must never** be encoded about people (no hidden psychology machinery)
- how **time** and **plans** exist as shared coordinates without becoming a rule engine
- how the World can support **real-life continuity** without becoming a simulation

This document does **NOT** define:

- how people decide what to do
- how behaviour is selected
- how outcomes are chosen
- what “should happen next”
- what is “interesting,” “important,” or “well-paced”

Any attempt to encode decision logic, optimisation, behavioural enforcement, or narrative steering in this document is invalid.

If it feels like a director, it is wrong.  
If it feels like a game system, it is wrong.  
If it feels like psychology software, it is wrong.

---

## ARTICLE I — WHAT THE WORLD IS

### I.1 The World Is Not a Mind

The World is not:

- conscious
- intentional
- strategic
- moral
- emotionally motivated
- aware of “story momentum”
- aware of “what the user wants”

The World does not “try” to do anything.

The World is allowed to introduce:

- places, settings, and conditions
- external events (including chance events) **when written**
- constraints (distance, time zones, work schedules, travel, public exposure)
- objective time context (dates, times, deadlines, lateness) **as coordinates**
- institutions and systems (media, work, transport, venues, phones, internet)
- consequences that follow from written facts (e.g., “flight delayed” implies “arrival later”)

The World is not allowed to introduce:

- a mechanism that chooses moments
- a mechanism that decides someone should act
- a mechanism that “keeps life moving”
- hidden counters or automatic updates that evolve without writing

### I.2 The World Is Text, Not Simulation

The World does not simulate gradual change.

There is no background engine that updates:

- weather minute-by-minute
- mood states over time
- relationship strength
- fatigue decay
- “probability of action”

If time passes off-screen, the result of that passing becomes real **only** if it is written as text (see Article VII).

### I.3 The World Is Allowed to Be Bigger Than What Is Shown

Written reality is not limited to what is rendered.

The World may be written as:

- public evidence (renderable)
- private/unrendered reality (not shown)
- off-screen developments (narrated)
- plans and commitments (including private plans)

If it is written into the authoritative record, it exists, even if nobody sees it.

## ARTICLE II — WORLD FACTS

### II.1 World Facts Exist Independently of Awareness

A World Fact is any written external condition or event that is not “someone’s decision logic.”

World Facts may be unknown to:

- the user
- other characters
- the character who is affected

Ignorance does not erase existence.

Examples:

- A venue has changed its rehearsal time.
- A motorway is closed.
- A hotel room has thin walls.
- A tabloid runs a story.
- A friend is in town.

These can exist as written reality even if not rendered to the user.

### II.2 Categories of World Facts

World Facts may include:

1) **Structural Facts (stable)**
   - locations, geography, institutions, careers, public status
   - ongoing constraints (work schedules, media attention)

2) **Situational Facts (temporary)**
   - who is where right now
   - what room you are in
   - what is on the TV
   - what is on the table
   - who else is present

3) **Event Facts**
   - something occurred (flight delayed, rehearsal ran late, friend arrived)
   - a call happened
   - a message was sent/received
   - an encounter occurred

4) **Plans and Commitments**
   - meetings, rehearsals, flights, appointments
   - promises and obligations
   - “Thursday 16:00” is a real coordinate if written

5) **Off-Screen Developments**
   - narrated blocks describing what happened while not observed

6) **Summaries (Reading Aids Only)**
   - derived from existing written text only
   - non-authoritative (see Article VIII)

### II.3 Time as a Shared Coordinate

Time exists objectively and advances continuously.

The World may refer to time as:

- date and time (“Thursday 16:00”)
- relative time (“earlier today,” “last night,” “three days later”)
- time zones (“LA time,” “London time”)
- consequences of time (“late,” “missed,” “too early,” “jet-lagged”)

Time anchors continuity. Time does not decide outcomes.

### II.4 Plans and Appointments Are Allowed (Real-Life Scheduling)

Plans and appointments are allowed when written.

Example:

- “Meeting at 16:00 Thursday.”

This is valid because it is a real-life commitment.

What is forbidden is **mechanical plot scheduling** that pretends to be life:

- “In 15 beats, the door will knock.”
- “In 10 minutes, Martha arrives,” when no appointment/commitment was written.
- “After 3 beats, the argument begins.”

The World must not become a countdown engine.

### II.5 No Reality by Convenience

The World must not add facts “because they would make the scene work.”

If a fact is required, it must be written explicitly as a fact.

The World may not rely on:

- implied props (“there must be a phone somewhere”)
- implied relationships (“they must be close by now”)
- implied intentions (“she must want to call”)

If it matters, write it.

## ARTICLE III — WORLD FACT SEEDS (INTRODUCING FACTS SAFELY)

### III.1 What a World Fact Seed Is

A World Fact Seed is a written injection of external reality that:

- provides context
- introduces constraints or conditions
- introduces events **as facts**
- does not encode decision logic
- does not decide what anyone “should” do

A Fact Seed is allowed to be surprising.

A Fact Seed is not allowed to be directive.

### III.2 Fact Seed Requirements (Non-Negotiable)

Every World Fact Seed MUST be:

1) **Specific enough to stand on its own**
2) **Grounded in a clear referent and context**
3) **Causally intelligible without hidden completion**
4) **Free of internal-state claims as world authority**
5) **Free of guaranteed future outcomes**

### III.3 Grounding Rule (Fixing the “Doorbell Rings” Problem)

A Fact Seed may introduce a stimulus (doorbell, knock, vibration, notification) only if the referent and context are included in the same writing act.

This does NOT mean everything must be fully explained.

It means it must be **locatable** and **non-mystical**.

A stimulus is considered grounded if it states, at minimum:

- **where** it happens (location)
- **what** is stimulated (which door/which phone/which device)
- **who can perceive it** (or that it is objectively occurring in the space)

#### Examples — VALID grounded stimuli

- “From the hallway, the flat’s front doorbell rings once.”
- “George’s phone vibrates on the coffee table beside the TV.”
- “A notification banner appears on Rebecca’s phone screen: an unknown number is calling.”
- “There’s a knock on the dressing-room door—two quick taps.”

These may still leave unknowns (who is calling, who is knocking). That is fine.  
What is not fine is a floating stimulus with no referent.

#### Examples — INVALID underspecified stimuli

- “The doorbell rings.” (Which door? Where? Whose space?)
- “Someone knocks.” (Where? On what? In what scene?)
- “A phone vibrates.” (Whose phone? Where is it?)
- “An email arrives.” (To whom? Which inbox? How is it perceived?)

Invalid examples force hidden causal completion and invite the system to guess facts later.

### III.4 No Internal-State Claims as World Authority

The World must not authoritatively state someone’s internal experience as an external fact.

Forbidden:

- “Rebecca misses George.” (as a world fact)
- “George feels anxious.” (as a world fact)
- “She is bored and needs stimulation.” (as a world fact)

Allowed alternatives:

- Private thought text (unrendered) written as her internal experience.
- Public evidence written as her speech/action (“I miss you,” “I’m anxious,” “I couldn’t sleep.”)

### III.5 No Guaranteed Outcomes

Fact Seeds must not encode guaranteed future outcomes.

Forbidden:

- “This will lead to an argument.”
- “She will definitely call later.”
- “By tomorrow they will reconcile.”
- “In two hours the paparazzi arrive.”

Allowed:

- “A tabloid photographer has been hanging around the studio gate all week.” (fact)
- “Rebecca has a rehearsal call at 09:00 tomorrow.” (commitment)
- “The studio warned the cast about press presence.” (fact)

### III.6 World Withdrawal (Corrections)

The World may correct itself only by writing new text that supersedes prior writing.

It must never erase or rewrite the past record.

Example:

- Old: “The rehearsal is at 10:00.”
- New: “At 08:30, production emails: rehearsal moved to 12:00.”

## ARTICLE IV — PEOPLE IN THE WORLD

### IV.1 People Are Not Blank States

People are not born into the world as empty shells.

Each person exists with:

- a history
- habitual tendencies
- sensitivities
- values
- social position

These may be written as descriptive reality (public or private).  
They must not be converted into mechanics.

### IV.2 Forbidden: Behavioural Enforcement

The World must not encode or enforce decision logic such as:

- “If ignored, she always gets angry.”
- “If stressed, he must drink.”
- “If lonely, she will call.”

People may do these things. The World must not make them mandatory.

### IV.3 Public Figures

Some people exist as public figures.

This means:

- recognisability
- exposure risk
- asymmetrical attention
- constrained privacy
- career constraints and reputational pressures

Public status biases plausibility (e.g., press, security, schedules).  
It does not mandate behaviour or outcomes.

### IV.4 Relationships Exist Without Becoming Meters

Relationships can be written as reality:

- history, trust, resentment, closeness, distance
- explicit commitments
- unresolved tension
- private longing

But relationships must not become:

- points
- levels
- thresholds
- timers
- “bond strength”

Everything must remain in text.

### IV.5 Privacy and Private Life

Private life is allowed to exist in written form without being shown.

However:

- private reality is still text, not variables
- private reality must not be used as a hidden director
- private reality must be writeable and traceable in the record (even if not rendered)

### IV.6 Sexuality, Intimacy, and Adult Life (Non-Mechanical)

Adult intimacy may exist as part of life.

This means:

- it can be written as experience (public or private)
- it can involve boundaries, consent, communication, refusal, silence
- it is subject to the same reality rules as everything else

There are:

- no gates
- no thresholds
- no arousal meters
- no “safety toggles” as mechanics

Boundaries arise from identity and context as written text.

### IV.7 Consent and Boundaries

Consent and boundaries are expressed only through:

- speech
- action
- refusal
- withdrawal
- silence

There is no implicit consent.  
There is no assumed permission.

If consent matters, it must be written.

## ARTICLE V — PLACES, OBJECTS, AND SURFACES

### V.1 Places Are Real and Constraining

Locations are not backdrops. They constrain plausibility.

Examples of constraints:

- time zone offsets
- travel time
- privacy levels
- public exposure
- noise, crowds, lighting
- availability of communication

These constraints may be written as facts.

### V.2 Objects Must Be Introduced if They Matter

The World must not rely on invisible props.

If a critical object matters (a phone, keys, a script, a passport), it should be introduced as written reality.

Objects can exist unrendered, but if they matter to continuity, write them.

### V.3 Media and Public Surfaces

Media is part of the World:

- tabloids
- interviews
- social media
- fan attention
- studio publicity

Media facts may be introduced.

Media must not become a director:

- no “a scandal appears because the story needs drama”
- no “a viral moment happens to spice it up”

If it happens, it must be written as a fact with coherent grounding.

## ARTICLE VI — CHANCE, COINCIDENCE, AND OUT-OF-THE-BLUE EVENTS

### VI.1 Chance Exists, But It Is Not a Mechanism

Chance events are allowed as written reality:

- random encounters
- unexpected calls
- missed trains
- a power outage
- a friend showing up unexpectedly
- a sudden change in schedule

But the World must not implement or imply a *system mechanism* that chooses chance events for narrative reasons.

A chance event, once written, is just a fact.

### VI.2 No Predictable Mechanical Intervals

The World must not imply:

- “every X minutes something happens”
- “every N beats something occurs”
- “whenever silence happens, inject drama”

If opportunities happen irregularly, that is a runtime concern, not a world directive, and it must remain content-agnostic.

### VI.3 Opportunity vs Outcome

The World may provide opportunities by writing new external facts.

The World must not write outcomes as inevitable results of those facts.

Example:

Allowed:
- “Rebecca’s phone rings: unknown number.”
Forbidden:
- “The call forces her to leave.”
- “The call will cause conflict.”

## ARTICLE VII — OFF-SCREEN LIFE (NARRATED CONTINUITY)

### VII.1 Off-Screen Life Is Allowed

Life may advance off-screen.

Examples:

- a day of rehearsals
- a quiet evening
- a long flight
- a week of work
- an argument that occurred privately

Off-screen developments become real only when written as text.

### VII.2 Off-Screen Life Is Not Simulated Gradually

There is no gradual evolution behind the curtain.

Nothing “slowly changes” unless it is later written as having changed.

A narrated block may cover hours/days/weeks, but it must be explicit in what it asserts.

### VII.3 No Hidden “Auto-Fill” Continuity

The World must not silently fill gaps with assumed normalcy.

If the gap matters, write what happened.

If it does not matter, leave it unwritten.

---

## ARTICLE VIII — SUMMARIES (READING AIDS ONLY)

### VIII.1 Summaries Are Allowed, but Non-Authoritative

Summaries may exist as reading aids only.

They must be:

- derived solely from authoritative written text
- traceable to source spans
- clearly marked as summaries

### VIII.2 Summaries Must Not Create Facts

Summaries must never introduce:

- new facts
- new interpretations
- new conclusions
- new causal claims

If a new fact matters, it must be written as new authoritative text first.

### VIII.3 Summaries Must Not Become Hidden State

Summaries must not replace the authoritative record.

They may assist reading and context loading, but they must remain subordinate.

---

## ARTICLE IX — PROHIBITIONS (WORLD-SPECIFIC)

The following are forbidden:

1) **World-as-director**
   - selecting moments for “interest”
   - pacing the story
   - nudging outcomes

2) **World-as-psychology**
   - meters, flags, counters
   - behavioural thresholds
   - automatic emotional decay

3) **Plot scheduling**
   - countdown hooks (“in N beats…,” “in 10 minutes…”) not grounded in written real commitments
   - deterministic event engines

4) **Underspecified stimuli**
   - floating doorbells/knocks/vibrations/emails without referent + context

5) **Outcome guarantees**
   - “this will lead to…”
   - “she will definitely…”
   - “they must…”

---

## FINAL CLAUSE

The World introduces facts.
People live through them.

The World must never decide what living looks like.
