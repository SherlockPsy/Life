## 0. Authority, Scope, and Relationship to LIFE_LAWS

### 0.1 Authority

This document defines the **Arbitration Laws of Life**.

These laws govern **how lived experience is selected, surfaced, delayed, suppressed, or reshaped** from the underlying reality defined in `LIFE_LAWS.md`.

They do not define what exists.
They define **what becomes experienced, when, and why**.

### 0.2 Scope

These laws apply continuously whenever:
- time advances,
- multiple truths coexist,
- multiple pressures compete,
- or lived experience is formed.

They operate across **all six life substrates** and all first-order laws.

### 0.3 Precedence

If a conflict arises:
- `LIFE_LAWS.md` defines **reality**
- `ARBITRATION_LAWS.md` defines **experience**

Mechanics, prompts, and implementations must comply with both.

## 1. Nature of Arbitration

Arbitration is:
- biased
- unstable
- non-optimal
- context-sensitive
- capacity-limited

Arbitration is **not deliberation**.
It is not rational optimisation.
It is not fair.

Any system that treats arbitration as logical choice **violates these laws**.

# THE ARBITRATION LAWS

---

## A1 — Uneven Salience
### (“Not All Truth Is Equally Loud”)

At any moment, many realities are simultaneously true.

Only some are salient.

Salience is not proportional to:
- importance
- correctness
- long-term consequence
- moral weight

Salience is influenced by:
- emotional charge
- bodily state
- recency
- context
- unresolved tension
- coincidence
- vulnerability

Minor realities may dominate experience.
Major realities may remain latent indefinitely.

Any system that consistently surfaces the “most important” thing **violates this law**.

---

## A2 — Non-Linear Threshold Crossing
### (“Awareness Is Not Gradual”)

Latent pressures accumulate invisibly until a threshold is crossed.

Thresholds are:
- individual
- state-dependent
- unstable
- non-linear

A trivial stimulus may surface:
- long-standing tension
- disproportionate emotion
- delayed reaction

There is no smooth mapping between cause and experience.

---

## A3 — Contextual Dominance
### (“What Wins Depends on Where and When”)

When multiple pressures compete, dominance is determined by context.

Context includes:
- physical setting
- social presence
- time constraints
- energy level
- exposure risk
- perceived consequences

No pressure permanently dominates all others.

Dominance may oscillate across time without resolution.

Any fixed priority hierarchy **violates this law**.

---

## A4 — Capacity Saturation
### (“Overflow Changes Arbitration Itself”)

When internal capacity is saturated:
- arbitration becomes crude
- nuance collapses
- avoidance increases
- habit dominates
- simplification replaces reflection

Saturation alters *how* arbitration works, not just *what* is chosen.

---

## A5 — Parallel Thread Persistence
### (“Unattended Life Does Not Pause”)

Life consists of **multiple simultaneous, persistent threads**.

Attention selects experience, not existence.

Threads that are not surfaced:
- continue to evolve
- accumulate pressure
- decay
- transform

They may resurface much later with altered weight.

Any system that pauses or freezes unattended threads **violates this law**.

---

## A6 — Non-Conscious Memory Binding
### (“Knowing Without Thinking”)

Memory influences behaviour without requiring recall.

Preferences, aversions, habits, and sensitivities may:
- bias behaviour
- shape interaction
- guide response

without being consciously remembered or narratively accessible.

Recall is optional.
Influence is not.

Any system that requires explicit recall for memory effects **violates this law**.

---

## A7 — Embodied Rhythms
### (“The Body Overrides the Mind”)

Some internal changes are:
- bodily
- rhythmic
- cyclical
- hormonally driven
- fatigue-driven
- illness-driven

These changes may override:
- rational arbitration
- intention
- habit
- identity

Embodiment is not an exception.
It is a primary driver.

Any system that treats internal life as purely cognitive **violates this law**.

---

## A8 — Path Dependence and Social Gravity
### (“Life Follows Existing Tracks”)

Life tends to continue along existing paths because:
- alternatives are costly
- alternatives are invisible
- alternatives require disruption

Repeated proximity produces familiarity.
Familiarity produces continuity.

Change is possible, but friction is real.

Any system that makes change easy by default **violates this law**.

---

## A9 — Quiet Value Recalibration
### (“What Feels Normal Changes Slowly”)

Values, tolerances, and boundaries shift gradually through:
- repeated exposure
- adaptation
- constraint
- fatigue
- acceptance

These shifts often occur without:
- reflection
- decision
- awareness

This is not mood.
This is not identity.
This is norm internalisation.

---

## A10 — Ambient Social Influence
### (“Atmosphere Has Force”)

Social influence does not require interaction.

Mood, tension, fear, or confidence may spread through:
- presence
- observation
- shared context
- perceived norms

People respond to **emotional weather**, not just actions.

Any system that limits influence to direct interaction **violates this law**.

---

## A11 — Narrative Substitution
### (“Coherent Lies Beat Incoherent Truths”)

Humans prefer explanations that:
- feel coherent
- reduce discomfort
- avoid confrontation

False causal narratives may stabilise indefinitely if they:
- fit emotional experience
- explain tension
- avoid cost

Reality does not automatically correct interpretation.

---

## A12 — Retrospective Reweighting
### (“The Past Changes Later”)

Past events may gain or lose significance long after they occurred.

This reweighting may occur:
- without new facts
- without new actions
- without conscious intent

Meaning is reassigned retroactively.

Memory is not static.

---

## A13 — Habituation and Desensitisation
### (“Repetition Can Remove Weight”)

Repeated exposure may:
- reduce emotional charge
- normalise intensity
- dull reaction

Accumulation does not always add weight.
Sometimes it subtracts it.

Both accumulation and desensitisation must coexist.

---

## A14 — Stable Incompletion
### (“Life Does Not Finish Sentences”)

Many arcs remain:
- unresolved
- unspoken
- unfinished
- suspended

Incompletion can stabilise indefinitely without pressure to resolve.

Narrative closure is not required for equilibrium.

Any system that forces resolution **violates this law**.

---

## A15 — Temporal Density Preservation
### (“Time Must Feel Like Time”)

Time must not be compressed for narrative convenience.

Long durations may contain:
- few events
- repetitive activity
- sustained interaction
- lived presence

Two hours may feel like two hours.

Any system that collapses time into highlights **violates this law**.

---

## 16. Arbitration Law Completeness Statement

This set of arbitration laws is considered **complete as a governing basis** for:

- salience
- dominance
- surfacing
- delay
- suppression
- reinterpretation

No additional arbitration law may be introduced unless:
- a scenario cannot be explained by these laws, **and**
- the gap cannot be resolved by parameterisation or mechanics.
